Bhavali

Bhavali Dam, is an earthfill dam on Bham river near Igatpuri, Nashik district in state of Maharashtra in India. Bhavali Dam is located near village Bhavali on Darana river tributary of Godavari river. The height of the dam above lowest foundation is 33.97 m (111.5 ft) while the length is 1,550 m (5,090 ft). The volume content is 329 km3 (79 cu mi) and gross storage capacity is 75,050.00 km3 (18,005.45 cu mi).[1]

Karanjwan

The volume content is 1,960 km3 (470 cu mi) and gross storage capacity is 175,580.00 km3 (42,123.88 cu mi).[2]

Vaitarna

The Vaitarna River (IAST: Vaitarṇā, pronunciation: [ʋəit̪əɾɳaː]) is a river in Nashik and Palghar district of Maharashtra. The Tansa is its left bank tributary and the Pinjal, Dehraja, and Surya are its right bank tributaries. Upper stretches of the Vaitarna are clean but in lower stretches it is polluted due to untreated industrial and civic waste. The Vaitarna is one of the most polluted rivers in India.[1]

The Crisis

  • Water storage is a fundamental aspect of water resource management, especially in regions prone to seasonal fluctuations in rainfall and areas dependent on agriculture. Effective water storage systems help in mitigating the impacts of droughts, improving agricultural productivity, and ensuring water availability for diverse needs throughout the year.

  • Over time, dams, rivers, and reservoirs accumulate sediment that reduces their water storage capacity and can impact water quality and flow. This sedimentation can lead to reduced efficiency in water management systems, posing a significant problem in water-scarce areas.

Our Impact
Fertile Land

Silt from dams often contains nutrients like phosphorus, potassium, and nitrogen, essential for plant growth. By testing the desilted material for nutrient content and potential contaminants, farmers can determine if it's suitable for use as a soil amendment. When appropriately applied, this nutrient-rich silt can improve soil fertility and crop yields.

Increased Water Storage

Over time, sediment such as silt, sand, and debris accumulate in reservoirs due to erosion and runoff from surrounding land. This sedimentation reduces the volume of water the reservoir can hold. Desilting involves excavating and removing these sediments, thereby creating more space within the reservoir to retain water.

Protection of Infrastructure

As sediment accumulates in a reservoir, it can reduce the available storage capacity behind the dam. This sedimentation not only decreases the volume of water the reservoir can hold but also affects the dam's ability to control water flow. Sediment can clog outlets, intake structures, or spillways, impeding the dam's ability to release water efficiently during heavy inflows.

Improved Water Quality

Desilting removes sediment from water bodies, improving water quality by reducing contaminants and turbidity. Cleaner water supports safe drinking supplies, efficient irrigation systems, and optimal conditions for industrial processes. Additionally, clearer water fosters healthier aquatic ecosystems by promoting sunlight penetration and reducing the risk of algal blooms.
